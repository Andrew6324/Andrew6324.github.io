Nancy Huang
Regarding China’s concealment of the severity of coronavirus

In the midst of the coronavirus outbreak, China has been outed by many for concealing the extent and severity of the virus, and reporting lower numbers of cases than the actual total amounts of outbreaks. Officials from the U.S. intelligence community have concluded that China’s public reporting on the number of cases and deaths that China had reported to the world were intentionally incomplete. Vice President Mike Pence says “The reality is that we could have been better off if China had been more forthcoming, what appears evident now is that long before the world learned in December that China was dealing with this, and maybe as much as a month earlier than that, that the outbreak was real in China”.
 China eventually imposed a strict lock down policy in China that has effectively slowed down the amount of cases reported, but the numbers they have reported continue to be questioned by both those inside and outside the country. Asymptomatic cases were not even included in the numbers at all until a month ago. Senator Ben Sasse, a Nebraska Republican, said in a statement after Bloomberg News published its report- “Without commenting on any classified information, this much is painfully obvious: The Chinese Communist Party has lied, is lying, and will continue to lie about coronavirus to protect the regime.”. In response to these comments, Hu Xijin, editor-in-chief of China’s state-run Global Times says “There was no way for serious data faking to occur in today’s China, especially for an incident that has drawn such widespread attention”.

 Even so, China has been accused of using the World Health Organization (WHO), to aid in concealing critical information about the virus. It was noted that many of the statements that WHO gave regarding the virus echoed those that Chinese officials gave or vice versa. For example, the day that WHO reported that there was no proof for human to human transmission regarding coronavirus, Wuhan officials reported the same information hours later. China has a great political influence over WHO, which makes it vulnerable to misinformation. The WHO receives funding from China and is dependent on the regime of the Communist Party on many levels. Due to this, the WHO has regurgitated misleading information given by Chinese authorities. 

The organization White house trade advisor Peter Navarro says “This was a time where that virus could have been contained in Wuhan, instead, 5 million Chinese people went out from Wuhan and propagated the virus around the world.”. By the time that Chinese officials have confirmed that coronavirus could be transmitted through human to human contact, it was too late. When asked how many people have been put in quarantine, isolation, or residential restriction, a representative of WHO referred to numbers from China’s National Health Commission-which are much smaller than the numbers calculated by the New York Times. It is clear that data given by WHO is unreliable due to the influence that China poses over them. As a result, Trump has sought to cut off US funding to the organization. 
       US Secretary of State Mike Pompeo says “China poses a threat to the world by hiding information about the origin of the coronavirus that it allowed to spread to other countries.”. China has the responsibility of being truthful in releasing information regarding coronavirus. As the virus did, in fact, originate in China, it is imperative that China releases information regarding the origins and spread of the virus.
       Though it is important to recognize that Chinese officials were at fault for concealing information about the outbreak when it first happened, it is equally important to recognize that the Chinese people, and Asian Americans are unrelated to this and not at fault. As the number of cases regarding racism against Asian Americans go up, Asian Americans are put in danger by the misinformation and anti-Asian sentiments that are magnified by the use of words like Chinese virus.

Pengfei Song
Amid the pandemic caused by the COVID-19, the health issues of all people in the United States is at stake. However, behind the danger on the surface, misinformation, another threatening enemy is lingering in the dark. In specific, misinformation toward Asians is prevalent nowadays which inevitably lead to racist language and actions toward this group of ethnicity. According to the report from Asian Pacific Policy and Planning Council (A3PCON), Chinese for Affirmative Action (CAA), the STOP AAPI HATE report center has received more than 1100 reports of coronavirus discrimination across the country during its first two weeks. Though the virus is originally discovered in China where Asians are the primary ethnicity, it does not justify the fact that Asians are currently becoming a target of assault, both verbally and physically, in the U.S.

First, it is clear that the Chinese government is slow on reaction to the original outbreak in Wuhan which contributed to a large part of the following global outbreak. In fact, in later December 2019, a doctor in China, Wenliang Li, had already filed reports on patients cases caused by an unknown strand of coronavirus. However, the Chinese government is reluctant to take radical actions toward the issue. It was reported later in 2020 that after the Li made the warning of the virus, he was quickly muted by governmental agencies and was called to report at the local police station for spreading un-proven information that would potentially cause the public to panic. Aside from censoring the source of the information, under the political environment of the spring festival, and an important and on-going governmental meeting, China had made more moves to further hide the existence of the approaching threat. The primary goal of the Chinese government at that time was to hide instead of taking caution. Between January 6th and 27th, only a few hundreds of cases were reported which in large misinform the rest of the world about the level of threat of the virus. The government only started to accelerate in a movement when the position is getting out of hand. On January 23rd, Wuhan, where the virus is originally discovered, was locked down by the Chinese government. However, a rather late action taken had failed to stop the virus from spreading to the world. At the time, people in Wuhan had already traveled to different areas of the world include the continent of America and Europe. The lockdown was mostly an utterly inadequate method in dealing with the severe situation.

Though China bears the most responsibility in failing to prevent the virus from spreading, the common enemy is still the virus itself instead of Asians, the group of ethnicity. After the situation accelerated in the U.S, misinformation about the virus quickly changed the way many people react to Asians. A lot of people started to believe that Asians should take full responsibility for the existence of the virus and the consequences caused by it. The racism toward Asians was quickly formed and it was contributed by both the public and governmental officials. First, Trump’s insistence use of the “Chinese virus” had really stirred up the flame. Though the WHO in February had already named the diseases COVID-19, Trump still chooses to call the diseases with a term that relates to certain groups of ethnicity. Though Trump had twitted about a call for protection of Asian Americans later, he still was in defense of using the term: Chinese virus, The Secretary of State Mike Pompeo’s uses of term–Wuhan Virus–also shifted blame onto anyone who bears the look of Asians. Public figures clearly had forgotten their role and ability in causing bigotry. As on April 28th, more than 30% of the Americans had witnessed bias toward people of Asian descent. Though 19% of Americans are currently sheltered in place. The incidence of attacks of all kinds toward Asians is still taking place.

The situation is especially serious in New York where currently is the epicenter of COVID-19. In New York City, an Asian woman wearing a mask was kicked and punched in the subway station. A man in Queen was followed and punched in the head in front of his 10 years-old son in a bus station. In the week of April 11th, a 51-year-old woman was attacked by teens and thrown on the “anti-Asians” statement. Similarly, a 59-year-old was kicked by a 13-year-old teen in New York City for the same racist motives.

The video above shows an Asian American woman got kicked in the face by 3 teenagers in Minnesota. (Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UOg-nNyaUZ4)

Cases of attacked flamed by anti-Asian bigotry also occurred in other states in the U.S. In Texas, a 2-year-old, and 6-year-old were stabbed because their family is Chinese and they were alleged of spreading the virus. In California, a 16-year-old Asian student was attacked in school by bullies who accused him of spreading the virus. He was sent to the emergency room after the incident.


The attack had also spread to people in medical positions who are in the front-line of fighting the virus. Dr. Edward Chew, who is the head of the emergency department at a large Manhattan hospital, reported notices of medical workers started to cover their nose and mouth when approached by patients incautious of potential attacks. During his free time, he went to Home Depot and bought some protective gear such as goggles and face shields. When he was walking to the parking lot, he was followed and harassed by three men in their 20’s.
Due to the rise in cases of attacks and racist deeds toward Asians, Asians had become the primary source of costumers in the purchase of weapons. “Gun shop owners in the Washington, D.C., area said they were seeing a surge of first-time Chinese-American buyers.” It’s also reported that In the first week of March, most of the customers in gun shops were Chinese-Americans or Chinese, who rarely visited weapon shops before the pandemic. 
Seeing the rise in numbers of people of Asian descent being attacked, it is crucial for us to ask why? Why did Asians become the target during the pandemic? Perhaps the issue is derived from the rooted racism toward Asian Americans in U.S History. 
The earliest laws regarding immigration policy explicitly discriminate against Asian. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 is a perfect example. According to the journal article, “The Chinese as Medical Scapegoats in San Francisco, 1870-1905,” Asians Americans also endure a history of being the scapegoats and discriminated against during pandemics in many cases. During the Smallpox outbreak in 1875, the government officials blamed the source on the unwholesome living condition of Asian Americans, especially on their food process habits, in Chinatown. Though there was no clear correlation proven. The discrimination can be seen from the fact that several hospitals at that time also refused to provide medical care to Chinese patients.
Judging from history, we can still see many of the same reasons for discrimination against Asian Americans appearing today. In specific, officials like Trump and Pompeo chooses to call the virus with a name that directly relates to a certain ethnicity. Regarding misinformation, since the origin of the virus can be traced to a market in Wuhan, people in the U.S again start to put discriminating tags on Chinese food. Ultimately, The current racism crisis toward Asian Americans is caused by both the non-doing of the government and misinformation conveyed during the time. 

The virus had already stranded us at home, it should not further divide us with racial discrimination. Fortunately, after the initial spike in the numbers of assault after Trump used the term Chinese Virus publicly in March and the enforcement of laws that ask residents to wear masks in public in several states like New York, the numbers of cases had gradually declined in the month of April. Compared to the initial reports of over 1100 cases received in March, in April only a few hundred more cases were reported. The decline in cases is also contributed by Asian celebrities who chose to stand out in public to make efforts to clarify. 


In the face of a global crisis, we should unit together with one intention to fight the virus as a whole and utilize laws and tools of clarification to prevent misinformation from hindering this collective goal.

On March 19th, Donald Trump was photographed, by a Washington Post photographer, having the word “corona” crossed out on his notes. Trump replaced the word with “Chinese” instead. According to NBC News, Trump denied the accusation of him being racist for using the word “Chinese Virus” instead of Coronavirus. Trump responds, “It comes from China, that’s why.” 
So why does Trump and his team insist on calling it “Chinese virus”? Americanprogress.org believes that “The goal of this effort is simple and dangerous: to erase the Trump administration’s responsibility for its deadly mishandling of the pandemic. This is a deft political strategy, because it is wrapped in genuine Chinese misdoing.” Indeed, Trump is trying to use China as a scapegoat for his failure to respond to the Coronavirus outbreak timely and effectively.
